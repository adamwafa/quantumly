States of Matter

Solids

  • Very difficult to compress

  • Cannot flow

  • Fixed shape

  • Fixed volume

Liquids

  • Difficult to compress

  • Can flow (fluid)

  • Takes the shape of the container

  • Fixed Volume

Gases

  • Easy to compress

  • Can flow (fluid)

  • Volume easily changes
    •



Molecular Model

Solids

  • Particles are in a regular, fixed arrangement

  • Particles are very close together (keeping them from going any closer,
    so they are incompressible), and they vibrate back and forth in fixed positions


  • Particles have strong attractive forces between them

Therefore, Solids keep their shape and are hard to break

Liquids

  • Particles are arranged randomly
  • Particles are relatively close together
    (making them incompressible), and they vibrate
  • Particles can flow over / past each other


  • Particles have weak attractive forces between them

Therefore, liquid particles can flow over each other

Gases

  • Particles are arranged randomly

  • Particles are very far apart (making them compressible),
    and they move randomly in all directions

  • Particles can flow over / past each other


  • Particles have negligible attractive forces between them

    • Therefore, gas particles can flow and fill up the container

Temperature

Is the measure of the avg. kinetic energy of particles in a substance 

Therefore, 
Higher temperature = particles gain K.E  
Lower temperature = particles loose K.E

Pressure

Particles in gas are constantly colliding with the walls of the container 
Therefore, the particles exert a force, then ultimately causing pressure

The Effect of temperature on pressure – Pressure Law 

For a fixed mass of gas at constant volume, 
pressure is directly proportional to temperature (when using Kelvin) 


At 0 ° C.
particles are still moving so there still is pressure 

At absolute zero, 0 kelvin, -273°C.
Particles are not moving, so they have no kinetic energy. No pressure is exerted


Math relation


Pressure (Pa) / temperature (°C) = constant 

P1 / T1 = P2 / T2



Brownian Motion

Random motion of gas particles by random molecular bombardment by other particles


  1. Small barely visible particles
  2. Zigzag and unpredictable path
  3. Move in groups

Evaporation

Is the process by which liquids turn into gas below the boiling point of the liquid 

Particles near the surface with the highest K.E can have enough energy  
to escape or evaporate from the liquid

After that, the avg. K.E of the remaining particles decrease

Therefore, the temperature decreases



Factors Affecting Evaporation


Temperature - increase in temperature increases rate of evaporation  

                    -  because it gives the particles more K.E to escape 


Surface area - increase in surface area increases rate of evaporation  

                    -  because it gives the particles more K.E to escape 


Wind Speed - increase in wind speed over surface increases rate of evaporation  

                  - because it gives the particles more K.E to escape 


Humidity - increase in humidity decreases rate of evaporation  

                -  because it gives less capacity to hold water vapor 


Atmospheric Pressure - increase in a.p decreases rate of evaporation 

                                      - particles require more K.E to escape


Pressure Changes


Boyles Law


States that “For a fixed mass at a constant temperature,pressure is inversely proportional to volume” 

As the volume increases, particles hit the walls less often, 

So avg. Force decreases, so pressure decreases

Pressure x volume = constant

P1 x V1 = P2 x V2
