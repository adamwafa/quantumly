Thermal Expansion

When objects are heated they expand

  • Heat applied, so temperature increases
  • Average K.E of particles increase
  • Particles vibrate and take up more space
  • ONLY the objects expand and not the actual particles

Rate of expansion is determined by the type of atom



Solids

  • They expand the least, usually too little to be visible by the naked eye

Consequences

  1. Railway tracks have to contain gaps to allow expansion
  2. Can cause wires tto snap
  3. Long sturctures like bridges need rollers


Liquids

  • Liquids expand more than solids,but less than gases
  • Expansions of liquid can be visible by the naked eye
  • This effect can be used in "liquid in gas" thermometers


Gases

  • Gases expand the most

  • Charle's Law - at constant pressure, the volume a gas is
    direcly proportional to its temperature


Volume / Temperature = constant
v1 / t1 = v2 / t2



Measurement of temperature

the expansion of a substance can be used to measure the temperature



Sensitivity

  • use a narrower tube,
    mosre distance for expansion
  • Use a material that expands more,
    small change in temperature = visible change in thread

Range

  • The maximum and minimum temperature in a thermometer,
    Low melting point , High melting point

Linearity

  • Weather or not expansion is directyly proportional to temperature,
    E.G water cannot be used, because its expansion is irregular


Thermocouples

A thermocouple is a commonly-used elictrical thermometer

  • It consists of 2 dissimilar couples joined together
  • When one junction is at a higher temperature an Electromotive force is generated,
    read by a voltmeter
  • Output voltage is proportional to temperature difference


  • Thermocouples can be very small, so they can heat and cool quickly,
    can measure rapidly varying temperatures
  • Thermocouples can measure the melting point up to the melting point of the metal


Thermal Capacity

As heat is supplied and the temperature of the body increase
the internal energy increases


The thermal capacity of an object is the
energy required to raise the temperature by 1°C



An object with higher thermal capacity needs more energy
to raise its temperature, therefore it takes longer to heat and cool



The temperature increase of a substance depends on :

  • Amount of energy applied
  • Mass of the substance
  • Nature of the substance


The specific heat capacity of a substance is the amount of energy (J) required
to raise 1kg of the substance by 1°C
Energy = mass x specific heat capacity x change in temperature



Experiment to determine the specific heat capacity of a substance

  1. Measure out 0.5kg of the substance and pour it
    into the beaker insulated by cotton
  2. Take the intial temperature of the substance with a thermometer
  3. Place SOW electrical heater into the water for 2 minutes
  4. Measure the temperature
  5. Use formula Q = mcT to find "c"


Melting & Boiling

For any pure substance undergoing a change of state,
There is an energy input without a change in temperature



Melting Point - Temperature at which solids change into liquids

Boiling Point - Temperature at which all liquids change into gases

Solidification - Process at which liquids change into solids

Condensation - Process at which gases turn into liquids


Difference between Evaporation and Boiling

  1. Boiling occure=s at a fixed temperture
  2. Evaporation can occur at any temperature
  3. Evaporation decreases temperaturee, while at Boiling temperature is constant



Latent Heat

  • Latent heat of fusion - Energy which must be put in a solid to at its melting people
  • Latent heat of vapourisation - energy which must be put in to vapourize into a liquid at its boiling point
  • Specific Latent Heat - Energy required to change the state of 1kg of a substance at constant temperature
